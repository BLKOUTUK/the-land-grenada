The location

Why Simon?

Simon isn’t remote — it’s positioned. The northeast is where Grenada’s next wave of investment is landing, and we’re already there.

Grenville

Parish capital and Grenada’s third-largest town — ten minutes away. Markets, services, transport, restaurants, the nutmeg processing station. The economic hub of the east coast.

Seamoon Industrial Park

12.5-acre GIDC business park less than a mile from the property. KM2 Solutions BPO employs 800+ people here. ICT, agro-processing, manufacturing. This is a working area, not a backwater.

Seamoon Cultural Centre

A $4M+ reconstruction into a first-class theatre and arts centre with training facilities. The historic home of Grenada’s calypso competitions. Cultural infrastructure arriving on our doorstep.

Pearls

The old airport sits adjacent to the property — visible on the satellite view. Now a heritage site and tourist attraction with its Cold War–era planes. The runway and its history draw visitors to the area daily.

Levera Development

A $1 billion resort under construction at the north of the island — 500 suites, golf course, casino, marina. The biggest tourism investment in Grenada’s history, pulling the entire northeast into focus.

Prospect Park

A first-class cricket ground right here in the parish. Combined with Simon as the site for the Grenada FA’s training facility, this puts serious sports infrastructure on our doorstep — and visiting teams need somewhere to stay.

St. Andrew is Grenada’s largest parish, and Simon sits at a convergence: cultural heritage, industrial development, and a billion-dollar tourism investment all within reach. The infrastructure is coming to us — we’re already on the ground.

The house has solid structure — 3 beds, 2 baths, kitchen, dining, verandahs on two sides, a staircase to the upper level. The bones are there. What it needs isn’t drastic — it’s a more modern use of space.

Right now: Costs money to maintain. Earns nothing. Sleeps 6 at best. But the structure is there — it just needs unlocking.

After setup

How the property runs itself

Once the house is set up and listed, nobody needs to send money. It covers its own costs and builds a reserve.

Local caretaker & cleaner

Someone from Simon or nearby who handles guest turnovers, cleaning, check-in, and basic maintenance. Employed from booking revenue — not a favour, a paid role.

Property manager

Manages the Airbnb listing, responds to guest queries, coordinates bookings. Can be local or remote. Once I’m on-island, I oversee this directly.

Maintenance fund

A percentage of every booking goes into a ringfenced fund for repairs, replacements, and upkeep. The pool, the deck, the appliances — all covered by the property’s own earnings.

Insurance & utilities

Electric, water, internet, property insurance — all paid from booking revenue. Running costs of ~$13K–16K/year are covered even at conservative occupancy.

The principle

After the initial fit-out, the property is an independent enterprise. It employs local staff, maintains itself, insures itself, and distributes any surplus to the family. Nobody subsidises it. If bookings drop, costs flex down. If bookings grow, income grows. The house works for us, not the other way round.

The target market isn’t individual couples. It’s groups.

At full capacity the retreat sleeps 36 guests — that’s three sports teams, or one large family reunion, or multiple retreat groups simultaneously. Small clusters of safari tents in the landscape, not a hotel block.

Simon already has a first-class cricket ground at Prospect Park and the Grenada FA training facility. Visiting teams need somewhere to stay — and we’re a five-minute walk away with group accommodation, catering, and a gathering space.

Beyond sports: family reunions, Carnival groups, diaspora reconnection trips. And courses, themed arts getaways, and healing retreats — the Seamoon Cultural Centre rebuild next door makes this a natural fit for creative and wellness programming.

A whole-property rental at $25–38 per person per night. A 4-bed villa with a pool, outdoor kitchen, and gathering space is rare in the Eastern Caribbean — nearly non-existent in St. Andrew.

The retreat uses part of the seven acres, not all of it. And land that sits unused in the Caribbean doesn’t stay unused forever — there’s development pressure, squatting, neglect. Active stewardship protects what the family owns.
